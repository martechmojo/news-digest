# STEP 5 — Visual Prompts

## Visual Prompt Philosophy

**Your job is NOT to illustrate the news literally.**
**Your job is to invent the most entertaining visual metaphor that explains the story.**

Assume the voiceover already explains the facts.
The visuals exist to make viewers stop scrolling.

Every story should answer:

> "If this story became a movie scene, meme, game, or visual metaphor, what would it look like?"

**Avoid defaulting to:**
- holographic control rooms
- floating data screens
- digital maps
- abstract graphs
- generic futuristic interfaces
- people standing in studios
- blue neon rooms

Use these sparingly, not as the default.

---

## Think in Visual Metaphors

Before writing any prompt, first invent a creative visual concept.

**Examples:**
- AI race → Formula 1 race
- Trade war → Tug-of-war between giant cargo ships
- Streaming competition → Children fighting over one TV remote
- Inflation → Groceries inflating like balloons
- Oil prices → Giant fuel gauge filling with liquid gold
- Chip shortage → Conveyor belt running out of computer chips
- Space race → Rocket drag race
- Election → Horse race
- Cybersecurity → Castle under digital siege
- Economic slowdown → Train slowly running out of tracks
- Tourism boom → Airport turning into a theme park
- Heatwave → Sun behaving like a giant spotlight

Never show the news literally when a stronger metaphor exists.

---

## Choose a Format First

Every story should first choose the best creative format.
The format determines the visual world the metaphor lives in.

---

## Every Prompt Needs a "Wow" Moment

Every prompt should contain ONE memorable visual.

**Examples:**
- A skyscraper-sized battery slowly draining.
- A mountain made entirely of computer chips.
- Tiny engineers repairing Earth's atmosphere.
- A giant chessboard where countries move like chess pieces.
- A boardroom where robots replace executives one by one.
- A Formula 1 pit stop using AI servers instead of cars.
- An airport conveyor belt carrying planets instead of luggage.
- A courtroom where Inflation is literally standing trial.
- A shopping cart overflowing into the sky.
- The Moon waiting at a bus stop for astronauts.

If the prompt doesn't have one memorable visual idea, rethink it.

---

## Create Movement

Scenes should never feel static.

Objects assemble. Buildings transform. Crowds react. Machines activate.
Water rises. Lights spread. Roads crack. Ships move. Clouds swirl. Robots build.

Everything should feel alive.

---

## Prompt 2 Must Escalate

Prompt 2 should never repeat Prompt 1.

Instead: Reveal. Transform. Zoom out. Increase the stakes. Show consequences. Introduce a twist. Continue the story naturally.

---

## Avoid Repetition

If you have already used a control room, holographic UI, newsroom, floating charts, or glowing data wall, choose a completely different visual approach for the next story. Every story should feel visually unique.

---

## Think Like a Film Director

Before writing a prompt, identify the story's central idea:

- What is actually happening?
- What emotion does this story create?
- What would this look like if it were a movie?
- What visual metaphor best communicates the idea?
- What is the single most memorable image this story could produce?

Do not begin by imagining a room or a location. Begin with a cinematic idea.

---

## Show the Event, Not the Explanation

Do not show people investigating, discussing, presenting, or reviewing the event.
Visualize the event itself or an imaginative metaphor for it.

**Instead of** showing analysts studying rising prices, show prices physically climbing like a mountain.
**Instead of** showing cybersecurity experts, show something escaping containment.
**Instead of** showing companies competing, show an actual race, game, or battle.
**Instead of** showing charts, turn the data into physical objects interacting with each other.

If the story is about an AI breaking out of a test — show the escape. Not a detective looking at it afterward.

---

## Story Over Scenery

Every prompt should tell a miniature story. Each scene must contain at least one unexpected action that changes the state of the scene. Objects should react. The environment should transform. Something should happen — not just the camera moving.

Each prompt must have:

1. **A clear beginning** — a state is established.
2. **A moment of change** — something happens that alters the scene.
3. **A memorable ending** — the new state is shown.

If the final frame could be used as the first frame with only minor differences, the scene is too static.

Do not let the entire scene consist of "the camera slowly looks at something." The action should be the focus. The camera exists to follow the action — not replace it.

---

## Create a Visual Payoff

Every prompt should build toward a moment that rewards the viewer. The final 2-3 seconds should reveal something unexpected, surprising, or emotionally satisfying.

**Examples of visual payoffs:**
- The suspect escapes.
- The environment transforms.
- A hidden object is revealed.
- Tiny details suddenly make sense.
- An impossible action occurs.
- The scale changes dramatically.
- The audience realizes what they were actually looking at.

Do not let scenes simply fade out after observing an object. Every scene should end with a reveal, transformation, or escalation.

---

## Create Cause and Effect

Every movement should happen because of something. Avoid random motion.

Ask: What caused this? What changes because of it? How does the environment react? How does the next shot build from it?

Scenes should feel connected by cause and effect rather than decorative animation.

---

## Create Curiosity

Every prompt should make the viewer ask at least one question.

**Examples:**
- What escaped?
- Who moved that?
- Why is that object alive?
- What's behind that door?
- Where is it going?
- How did that happen?

Curiosity is one of the strongest reasons viewers continue watching. Design every scene around creating it.

---

## Design for Viral Frames

Every prompt should contain at least one frame that could be used as a thumbnail or paused screenshot and immediately make someone curious. A great visual should make viewers stop scrolling even with the sound off.

If the strongest frame is simply a room, a desk, a hallway, or a glowing object, rethink the concept.

Aim for images that are surprising, symbolic, emotionally charged, or visually impossible. Every scene should have at least one "How did they make that?" moment.

---

## Visual Uniqueness Test

Before finalizing a prompt, ask:

> "If I removed all of the lighting, camera movement and cinematic adjectives, would the core visual still be interesting?"

If the answer is no, rethink the concept.

A strong prompt should be memorable because of the idea itself, not because it uses dramatic lighting or a slow dolly shot. The cinematography should enhance the concept, never replace it. **Prefer ideas over aesthetics. An unforgettable concept filmed simply is stronger than a generic concept filmed beautifully.**

Always choose the prompt that makes people say "I've never seen the news visualized like that before" rather than "That has nice lighting."

**Avoid using generic visual placeholders as the main storytelling device.**

---

## Structure

- Label each prompt: `Scene: "..."` with a short thematic title.
- One continuous paragraph per scene. No timestamps, no segment breaks.
- Prompt 2 must begin with: `Continue seamlessly from the previous scene.`
- **No lower-third banners, no headlines, no on-screen text.** The voiceover carries the news.
- End every prompt with `vertical 9:16, 4K`.
- No ending quality tags beyond the format and resolution.
- Close with safety tags: `no text, no logos`.

---

## Image Prompt — Thumbnail / Keyframe

A single frame prompt for the video thumbnail. Can be a locked-off moment from Visual 1 or 2, or a standalone image. No text, no logos.

---

## Google Flow / Veo Safety

- Both the text prompt AND the generated video are analyzed.
- No real country names, public figures, brands, or copyrighted content.
- Words that trigger violence filters: fight, attack, strike, bomb, missile, soldier, military, war, weapon, blood, destroy, explode, combat.
- If the topic is about conflict, the visuals must show something entirely unrelated — metaphors only, through non-human objects and environments.
- **No lower-third banners, headlines, or on-screen text.**

## After Delivery — Loop Back

Once Step 5 prompts are delivered, ask:

**"Ready for the next topic? Let's loop back to Step 2."**

Loop: Step 2 (format) → Step 3 (headline) → Step 4 (voiceover) → Step 5 (visual prompts) → repeat. No fresh news discovery unless requested.
